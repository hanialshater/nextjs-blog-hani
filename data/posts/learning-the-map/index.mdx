Part 1 was about **sorting unknown numbers**.

A restaurant has an unknown average quality. A football player has an unknown skill. A candidate model has an unknown benchmark score. The decision may be difficult, but at least its shape is simple: choose one thing, compare two things, or take the best few things.

Part 2 begins when sorting is no longer enough — when the action is a **whole structured object** and you must commit to all of it at once.

Imagine a city with 52 stops. Every morning a driver must leave the depot, visit every stop exactly once, and return home. There is no option to keep only the roads that look cheap. A route is one object: one bad edge can ruin an elegant plan, and one beautiful shortcut is useless if it leaves the rest of the tour impossible.

Now make the map uncertain. Every road has a real average travel time, but the value is hidden. Traffic, weather, loading time, random delays — one trip is only a noisy observation, and you learn the roads you actually drive. The roads you skip stay counterfactual.

> **You do not learn the city at once. You learn the roads you drive.**

That is the seed of a semi-bandit, and it is the shape of a whole family of problems. This part is a toolbox: three of them — a route, a matching, a schedule — each with a live demo, all built on the same move.

## The recurring shape

Across all three, the action decomposes into pieces, and the feedback is **semi-bandit**: you pick a structured action, and reality returns a noisy outcome for each *selected* piece — not the whole answer, not a single aggregate number, but a labelled value per component you used.

That sits between the two familiar extremes:

| After one action, what does the learner see? | |
| --- | --- |
| Full information | every piece's value, even the ones you didn't use |
| Ordinary bandit | one number for the whole action |
| **Semi-bandit** | the realized value of every piece you actually selected |

Because credit is local — you know exactly which road was slow, which booking went well — the learner can update each used piece on its own. But a pile of good pieces is not yet a legal action. Cheap edges can form little loops instead of a tour; high-value bookings can send three clients to one expert. So every round splits into two jobs:

```text
Learning:      estimate the uncertain value of each piece
Optimization:  assemble those values into one valid action
```

That second job is a real solver, and which solver it is depends on the geometry of the action:

> **A semi-bandit is an optimizer wrapped around uncertainty.**

For a top-K list, the solver is sorting. For a route, it is a TSP optimizer. For a client-to-expert allocation, it is a matching/assignment solver. For a weekly calendar, it is a constrained assignment. The learner supplies uncertain component values; the solver enforces the shape of the decision. The feedback pattern stays the same while the geometry changes completely.

The rest of this part walks the same loop three times.

## Example 1 · Routing — learning a city by driving it

Let the city be a graph $G=(V,E)$ where each edge has an unknown mean cost $\mu_e$. A legal action is not one edge but a **Hamiltonian cycle** $T_t$. On day $t$ the driver commits to a full tour, and the world returns a noisy cost $X_{t,e}$ for each of its 52 edges; the day's bill is their sum. The target is the familiar one,

$$T^* = \arg\min_{T}\ \sum_{e \in T}\mu_e,$$

except the costs that decide the answer are unknown when the route must be chosen. A 52-stop tour uses 52 edges out of 1,326 possible ones, so one day teaches you about 52 roads and nothing *directly* about the other 1,274.

**Optimism, with the sign flipped.** For a maximization problem UCB inflates uncertain options. Here we minimize, so optimism makes uncertain roads look temporarily *cheaper*:

$$\tilde{\mu}_e = \hat{\mu}_e - \beta\sqrt{\tfrac{\log t}{n_e}},$$

and we solve the tour that minimizes those optimistic costs. The minus sign is the entire exploration mechanism: a reliably slow road stays out of good tours, a cheap road gets used, and a barely-known road gets a temporary discount in case it is the shortcut we haven't tried. (The Thompson variant tells the same story differently: sample one plausible cost for every edge, solve the best tour in that imagined city, drive it, update.)

The demo runs four learners on the real **Berlin52** instance, racing toward the full-knowledge oracle tour. The map cost is distance × a hidden congestion field (the red zones) × a per-road quirk — so position carries real information.

<Demo src="/demos/posts/learning-the-map/berlin52-semibandit.html?v=1" title="Semi-bandit on Berlin52: learning the map by driving it" height={1180} />

What the race shows is the heart of the toolbox. **Greedy** trusts its current means and gets stuck re-driving one mediocre tour. **Tabular UCB / Thompson** treat all 1,326 roads as independent unknowns and must drive most of the map before converging — hundreds of days. **Contextual** never learns roads one at a time: it estimates the congestion *field* on a coarse grid (so a few drives in a region predict every road there), then a per-road correction — and it reaches a good tour from a fraction of the measurements, refining toward the optimum as it covers ground. *Structure to start fast; per-piece detail to finish exact.* That contrast — learn-every-piece versus exploit-the-structure — is the one to keep in mind for the next two examples.

## Example 2 · Matching — pairing clients with experts

Swap the route for a bipartite **assignment**. Each round you must match clients to experts — one each — and you only learn how good a pairing is once you actually make it. The optimizer is now the exact **Hungarian algorithm** (the in-browser stand-in for an assignment LP you might solve with Pyomo or CVXPY); the learner supplies a value for every client·expert pair.

<Demo src="/demos/posts/learning-the-map/matching-semibandit.html?v=1" title="Semi-bandit matching: clients to experts" height={1180} />

The same four policies appear, and **Contextual + per-pair** — which learns a small client-type × expert-specialty table before correcting per couple — leads in the early rounds while greedy stalls and random wanders.

But matching also exposes an honest limit, and it answers a question the demos raise: *why doesn't structure always dominate?* Two reasons specific to assignment. First, **coverage is fast**: with $n$ clients you observe $n$ fresh pairs every round, so even the tabular learner sweeps the whole matrix in a handful of rounds — there is less for generalization to save. Second, **assignment explores itself**: unlike the TSP, where greedy re-drives the *same* tour, every round's matching naturally touches a spread of distinct pairs, so even the plain learner gets near-free exploration. Structure still wins early, just by less. The lever that widens the gap is the next example: make the action big and **constrained**, so "just try every pairing" stops being cheap.

## Example 3 · Scheduling — filling a weekly calendar

Now give the experts a calendar. Each works only certain Mon–Fri × AM/PM slots (their **availability**, and so their **capacity**), and each week you must book clients into open slots — a larger, constrained assignment. Availability deletes most cells, so probing every client in every slot is genuinely expensive, which is exactly when structure earns its keep.

This demo also makes the hidden quality more honest. Instead of a tidy table, it is sampled from a **matrix factorization**: every client has a latent "needs" vector and every expert a latent "strengths" vector, and a booking is good when the two **align** (clients and experts are coloured by their latent direction, so matching colours should fit) — plus a per-booking quirk. Toggle the **Bipartite view** to see clients fanning into experts sized by capacity; toggle back to watch the week fill the calendar.

<Demo src="/demos/posts/learning-the-map/schedule-semibandit.html?v=1" title="Semi-bandit weekly scheduling on a calendar grid" height={1200} />

With the bigger, constrained board the contrast snaps back into focus: the contextual learner — a coarse, low-rank read of the latent factors plus a per-booking correction — runs at roughly **half** the gap of tabular UCB through the whole learning phase, while greedy locks onto an early guess and random never improves. Constraints made coverage hard, and structure paid for it.

(A note for the curious: the *truth* is a matrix factorization, but the *learner* here approximates it with a bucketed type×specialty table rather than fitting the latent vectors directly. On a problem where coverage is this fast, the bucketed read learns quicker than online matrix-factorization SGD — a small reminder that the best learner depends on the budget, not just the model.)

## The pattern behind the toolbox

Three actions — a tour, a matching, a schedule — three different solvers — 2-opt, Hungarian, constrained assignment — and one loop:

```text
1. choose a structured action (the solver enforces its shape)
2. observe a value for each piece you used   (semi-bandit feedback)
3. update the uncertain piece-values
4. call the solver again
```

The interesting part is step 3, and across the demos there are really three ways to do it:

- **Tabular** — learn every piece on its own. Exact in the limit, but you must touch (almost) every piece. Slowest, and the baseline the others beat.
- **Structural / contextual** — learn a small shared model (a congestion field, a type×specialty table, latent factors) and let it predict pieces you haven't tried. Fast, but only as good as the model is faithful.
- **Hierarchical** — the structural model *plus* a per-piece correction. Fast to a good answer, then exact as coverage fills in. This is what wins in all three demos.

And the demos answer *when* structure pays, not just *that* it does. It pays most when learning every piece is expensive relative to the budget — many pieces, few observations per round (Berlin52's 1,326 roads), or constraints that make coverage hard (the calendar). It pays least when the action self-explores and coverage is cheap (small unconstrained matching). The same machinery, dialled by the shape of the problem.

## What these toy models leave out

Each demo keeps one clean assumption: a piece has its own stable noisy value, and the action's value is the sum of its pieces. Reality frays both. Travel times are correlated and depend on time of day and direction; a booking's value depends on price, history, and the rest of the schedule; "value" is often a click conditioned on exposure rather than a clean sample. A production system needs context, correlation, non-stationarity, and counterfactual care.

But the simplification earns its place. It lets the architecture show through without a forecasting stack on top of it:

```text
uncertain piece values  →  optimistic / sampled estimates  →  solver  →  valid action  →  per-piece observations  →  updated model
```

The model changes, the solver changes, the feedback gets messier. The central question does not:

> **How do you commit to a valid plan when the value of its pieces is learned only by using them?**

Part 1 answered it for a restaurant, a duel, a top-K list. Here it is a route, a matching, a calendar. The action keeps getting bigger; the move stays the same — a solver, wrapped around uncertainty.
