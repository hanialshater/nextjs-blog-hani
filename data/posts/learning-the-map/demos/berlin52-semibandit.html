Semi-bandit on Berlin52 — learning the map by driving it

Every day you must drive a full valid tour of all 52 stops. Each road's cost is its length times the hidden congestion of where it runs (the red zones) times the road's own quirk, revealed only on roads you actually drive. Five policies race to the cheapest tour.

Thick faint edges on the map are roads this policy is still uncertain about; thin solid edges are well measured. Click a legend entry to show that policy on the map.

True road cost = straight-line distance (TSPLIB berlin52) times a hidden congestion field (the red zones); each drive returns that cost with multiplicative noise. The optimizer is nearest-neighbour + 2-opt on each policy's view of the map: random ignores the map, greedy trusts its current means, UCB makes unmeasured roads look temporarily cheaper (the minus sign in mean − β·√(log t / n)), and Thompson solves one plausible sampled map. True cost = distance × regional congestion × a per-road factor unique to each edge. The tabular policies treat all 1326 roads as independent unknowns, so they must drive most of the map to converge. Contextual + per-edge is hierarchical: it learns the shared congestion field on a coarse grid (so a few drives in a region predict every road there) and then a per-road correction as each edge is driven. It reaches a good tour far sooner than the tabular policies from the shared structure, and keeps refining toward the optimum as it covers roads — structure to start fast, per-edge detail to finish exact.
