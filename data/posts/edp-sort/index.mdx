Five restaurants are near your home. One is probably best on average.

If you could read their true qualities, this would be trivial: sort the numbers and take the maximum.

$$\arg\max_i \mu_i$$

But you cannot read them. You can try one tonight, another tomorrow, and another next week. Each dinner tells you something, but never enough. Perhaps the chef had a bad night. Perhaps you ordered badly. Perhaps the place is excellent and you found its one terrible dish.

So you are not really choosing a restaurant. You are **sorting unknown numbers**.

Each restaurant has a real average quality $\mu_i$. The values are hidden. A dinner is one noisy observation of one value. You need to learn enough of their order without spending all your dinners on bad restaurants.

That creates a trade-off. You can **exploit** what currently looks best, or **explore** a restaurant whose quality is still unclear. Explore too little and an early lucky dinner can lock you into the wrong answer. Explore too much and you keep paying for mediocre meals after the answer is mostly clear.

The question is not just “which restaurant is best?” It is:

> **Where should the next try go?**

The restaurant is the small version of a much broader problem.

To find a shortest path through a city, the graph may be known while travel times on its edges are uncertain and variable. You do not merely run Dijkstra on fixed weights; driving a road is how you learn its weight.

To match customers with experts, the feasible matches may be known while the quality of each pairing is not. Every match teaches you something about preferences and compatibility, but it also uses an opportunity.

To rank products, sellers, ads, or creators, the candidate set and objective may be known while the quantities that determine the right order are not. You learn from clicks, purchases, returns, comparisons, ratings—whatever feedback reality gives you.

In all of these cases, the optimization problem is known. The actions, constraints, and objective are clear. What is unknown are the parameters that determine which solution is best, and the observations are stochastic.

So the problem must be solved while its parameters are being learned.

## Part I — The one-armed bandit: choosing dinner

The restaurant problem is a **multi-armed bandit**: choose one option, observe one noisy outcome, update your belief, repeat.

Each restaurant is an **arm**. A visit is a **pull**. The dinner is the **reward**. You never see a restaurant's true average quality; you see one noisy dinner at a time, and only for the restaurant you chose. The others remain counterfactual.

### The cost of not knowing: regret

There are $k$ arms, each with an unknown reward distribution. At every round, choose one arm, observe one noisy reward, and try to maximize total reward. Equivalently, minimize **regret**:

$$\text{Regret}(T) = \sum_{t=1}^{T}\big(\mu^* - \mu_{a_t}\big), \qquad \mu^* = \max_i \mu_i$$

If the sushi place is truly best and you eat somewhere weaker tonight, the gap between their hidden average qualities is tonight's expected regret.

Commit after a handful of dinners and early noise can crown the wrong winner. Explore every restaurant evenly forever and you waste dinners after the answer is clear. A bandit policy is a rule for dividing your budget between learning and using what you have learned.

Before naming the policies, try it yourself.

There are five hidden restaurant qualities. You choose where to eat; a second policy chooses in parallel. The goal is not to win one dinner. It is to avoid accumulating bad dinners while you work out where the good ones are.

### Three policies cover most of the ground

- **ε-greedy.** Choose the current best arm 90% of the time and a random arm for the other 10%. The random part is insurance against an early mistake, but it keeps paying that 10% after the winner is obvious.

- **UCB1** — *optimism in the face of uncertainty.* Score each arm by

  $$\hat{\mu}_i + \sqrt{2\ln t / n_i}$$

  and choose the largest score. The first term is what you believe; the second is how wrong you might still be. Barely tried arms get a bonus that shrinks as evidence arrives.

- **Thompson sampling** — *probability matching.* Keep a belief distribution for each arm. Each round, draw one plausible value from every belief and choose the highest draw. A barely tried arm has a wide posterior and occasionally gets another chance. A known-bad arm has a narrow, low posterior and rarely wins.

<Demo src="/demos/posts/edp-sort/bandit-race.html" title="Race the bandit policies" height={560} />

Pure exploit fails because an early lucky result can become a permanent false belief. ε-greedy repairs that by buying random checks. UCB and Thompson improve on it because their checks go where uncertainty is still relevant.

UCB is not computationally slow; it is one score per arm. Its conservatism is statistical. The confidence bonus keeps revisiting arms that a more model-aware method might discard earlier. In practice, people often use tighter variants such as KL-UCB or UCB-Tuned, or Thompson sampling when a Bayesian reward model is natural.

<Demo src="/demos/posts/edp-sort/bandit-playground.html" title="Pull the bandit yourself" height={520} />

The good actions are not simply the high-reward ones. They are the actions that are worth both their immediate outcome and what they teach you.

### Context changes which arm is best

A quick lunch, a date, and a rainy solo dinner are different **contexts**. A plain bandit learns one global average per restaurant. A **contextual bandit** sees features of the situation before choosing.

In LinUCB, each arm learns weights $\theta_a$ over a context vector $x_t$, predicts $x_t^\top\theta_a$, and adds an uncertainty bonus. The question becomes: which restaurant is best for this situation, given what we know and what we still need to learn?

<Demo src="/demos/posts/edp-sort/bandit-contextual.html" title="Contextual dinner bandit" height={420} />

The basic bandit has one feedback channel: choose one item and observe one reward. Now change that. Suppose you can only compare two items and see which one wins.

## Part II — Sorting: when the arm is a pair

Eight players. No stat sheet. No clean skill number to read.

You can make any two play and watch who wins. A match is a coin whose bias depends on the skill gap: a strong player usually beats a weak one; evenly matched players are close to a toss-up.

How many matches do you need before the league table is reliable? More importantly: **which match should happen next?**

<Demo src="/demos/posts/edp-sort/pitch-match.html" title="A league table built from noisy 1v1 matches" height={660} />

This is sorting, except the comparison oracle is unreliable. In a textbook, you sort by reading numbers. Here, the better player only *probably* wins.

The standard model is the **Bradley-Terry** law:

$$P(i \succ j) = \sigma\!\big(\beta(s_i-s_j)\big)$$

Each player has a hidden skill $s_i$. The logistic curve turns the difference $s_i-s_j$ into a win probability. Equal skills give a 50/50 match; a large gap gives an almost certain result.

One match is one noisy observation of one pairwise difference. But the pairs are not independent. If A beats B repeatedly and B beats C repeatedly, you learn something about A versus C without scheduling that match. The pairs share the same hidden skill vector.

A pair separated by a gap $\Delta$ needs roughly $\log(1/\delta)/\Delta^2$ repeated matches before you can call the order with confidence $1-\delta$. Big gaps settle quickly. Near ties consume the budget.

### How the table learns

**Batch fit.** Given all matches so far, find the Bradley-Terry skill values that best explain them. Sort the fitted skills and you have a ranking. This is clean, but refitting from scratch after every match does not scale.

**Online fit: Elo.** Treat each match as one logistic-regression example and take a single SGD step. If $i$ beats $j$:

$$s_i \leftarrow s_i + \eta(1-p), \qquad s_j \leftarrow s_j - \eta(1-p), \qquad p = \sigma(s_i-s_j)$$

The update is the surprise, $1-p$. If the favourite wins, the ratings barely move. If an underdog wins, they move sharply.

The recipe has three parts:

> **estimate** skills from matches → **read off** the ranking by sorting → **schedule** the next match.

### Where to spend the matches

- A **lopsided** match produces an expected Elo update close to zero. It mostly confirms what you already knew.
- A **near-even** match is both uncertain and capable of changing the table.

Sample near the boundaries where the answer can still change.

- **Random** chooses any pair.
- **Round-robin** chooses the least-played pair, $\arg\min_{(i,j)}n_{ij}$.
- **Ladder** compares neighbours in the current table.
- **Active** chooses a pair that is both close and under-sampled:

  $$\arg\max_{(i,j)}\left[\text{closeness}(|\hat{s}_i-\hat{s}_j|)+\text{uncertainty}\left(\frac{1}{1+n_{ij}}\right)\right]$$

<Demo src="/demos/posts/edp-sort/pairing-race.html" title="Where to spend your matches" height={440} />

The curve is Kendall $\tau$, or table correctness, against matches played. Boundary-seeking schedulers settle the table first because they spend their budget where the order is still movable.

Set the table to **you scout (click 2)**. Pick two players, run a match, inspect the table, and choose the next pair. Obvious mismatches feel safe, but they rarely change anything.

### Why the candidate pool must be wider than K

Suppose you only need the **best K** items. Comparing only items currently in the top-K fails in three ways.

1. A genuinely top-K item currently sitting at K+1 receives no matches and has no path back into contention.
2. Inside a settled top-K, comparisons are often lopsided; Elo updates are near zero and the cutline does not move.
3. If the top group is isolated from the field, “K-th best” loses its anchor.

The fix is a **candidate band around the cutline**. Keep sampling everyone whose uncertainty still overlaps the K-th position. Let the band narrow only when evidence rules people out.

With **10,000** players there are about 50 million possible pairs. Use an online estimator such as Elo and an adaptive scheduler that focuses comparisons near the K-th rating. The metric is **recall@100**: how much of the true top-100 appears in the estimated top-100.

<Demo src="/demos/posts/edp-sort/topk-scale.html" title="10,000 players: find the best 100" height={500} />

Random pairing wastes matches far from the cutline. Adaptive matching recovers most of the top 100 in tens of thousands of matches rather than fifty million by concentrating effort where membership of the set is still uncertain.

## Part III — Combinatorial bandits: choosing a whole top-K

Everything so far used **comparison feedback**. You stage A against B and receive one bit: who won.

There is another setting where every selected item returns its own outcome: a click, watch time, purchase, or benchmark score. The goal may still be top-K, but the observation model is different.

- **Comparison feedback:** fit latent skills from “A beat B,” sort them, and schedule matches near the boundary.
- **Per-item reward feedback:** pick a slate and observe a reward for each selected item.

The second setting is a **combinatorial semi-bandit**. For each item, Combinatorial-UCB keeps an optimistic score:

$$u_i = \bar{x}_i + \sqrt{c\,\ln t/n_i}$$

Each round, choose the top-K by $u_i$, observe a reward for each selected item, and update those items.

The limitation is simple: **Comb-UCB cannot run on “who won?”** It needs a reward for each item. A football match does not provide one.

The next demo is not a fair race between two solutions to the same feedback problem. It shows two machines looking at the same hidden item quality through different observation channels.

<Demo src="/demos/posts/edp-sort/bt-vs-combucb.html" title="Bradley-Terry versus combinatorial-UCB: top-K recovery" height={400} />

Comb-UCB receives several direct rewards each round; Bradley-Terry receives one comparison bit. Bradley-Terry gives a global latent ranking, reusable for any K. Comb-UCB gives per-item reward estimates for the items it has selected, but says little about items it never observed.

Same goal. Different feedback. Different machinery.

## What this adds up to

The restaurant, the league table, and the top-K slate are variations on the same move.

The optimization structure is known. The parameters that determine the answer are not. Attempts reveal them only partially and noisily, so the solver must decide both what to do now and what to learn next.

For independent arms, that is a bandit. For pairwise comparisons, it is active ranking with a latent-skill model. For a slate with per-item rewards, it is a combinatorial semi-bandit.

The reusable question is still the first one:

> **Where should the next try go?**
