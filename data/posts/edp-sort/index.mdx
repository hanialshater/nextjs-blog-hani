Five restaurants are near your home. One is probably best on average.

If you could read their true qualities, this would be trivial: sort the numbers and take the maximum.

$$\arg\max_i \mu_i$$

But you cannot read them. You can try one tonight, another tomorrow, and another next week. Each dinner tells you something, but never enough. Perhaps the chef had a bad night. Perhaps you ordered badly. Perhaps the place is excellent and you found its one terrible dish.

So you begin by **sorting unknown numbers**.

Each restaurant has a real average quality $\mu_i$. The values are hidden. A dinner is one noisy observation of one value. You need to learn enough of their order without spending all your dinners on bad restaurants.

That creates a trade-off. You can **exploit** what currently looks best, or **explore** a restaurant whose quality is still unclear. Explore too little and an early lucky dinner can lock you into the wrong answer. Explore too much and you keep paying for mediocre meals after the answer is mostly clear.

The question is not just “which restaurant is best?” It is:

> **Where should the next try go?**

The restaurant is the small version of a broader problem. You begin by sorting unknown numbers; more generally, you are optimizing a known structure whose decisive parameters are unknown.

To find a shortest path through a city, the graph may be known while travel times on its edges are uncertain and variable. You do not merely run Dijkstra on fixed weights; driving a road is how you learn its weight.

To match customers with experts, the feasible matches may be known while the quality of each pairing is not. Every match teaches you something about preferences and compatibility, but it also uses an opportunity.

To rank products, sellers, ads, or creators, the candidate set and objective may be known while the quantities that determine the right order are not. You learn from clicks, purchases, returns, comparisons, ratings—whatever feedback reality gives you. But clicks and purchases are not pure item quality: they are outcomes conditional on exposure, rank, price, inventory, presentation, and the policy that chose the item. Logged feedback must therefore be treated with care.

In all of these cases, the optimization problem is known. The actions, constraints, and objective are clear. What is unknown are the parameters that determine which solution is best, and the observations are stochastic.

So the problem must be solved while its parameters are being learned.

## Part I — The multi-armed bandit: choosing dinner

The restaurant problem is a **multi-armed bandit**: choose one option, observe one noisy outcome, update your belief, repeat.

Each restaurant is an **arm**. A visit is a **pull**. The dinner is the **reward**. You never see a restaurant's true average quality; you see one noisy dinner at a time, and only for the restaurant you chose. The other restaurants remain counterfactual.

### The cost of not knowing: regret

There are $k$ arms, each with an unknown reward distribution. At every round, choose one arm, observe one noisy reward, and try to maximize total reward. Equivalently, minimize **regret**:

$$\text{Regret}(T) = \sum_{t=1}^{T}\big(\mu^* - \mu_{a_t}\big), \qquad \mu^* = \max_i \mu_i$$

If the sushi place is truly best and you eat somewhere weaker tonight, the gap between their hidden average qualities is tonight's expected regret.

Commit after a handful of dinners and early noise can crown the wrong winner. Explore every restaurant evenly forever and you waste dinners after the answer is clear. A bandit policy is a rule for dividing your budget between learning and using what you have learned.

There are five hidden restaurant qualities. You choose where to eat; a second policy chooses in parallel. The goal is not to win one dinner. It is to avoid accumulating bad dinners while you work out where the good ones are.

### Four policies cover most of the ground

- **ε-greedy.** Choose the current best arm 90% of the time and a random arm for the other 10%. The random part is insurance against an early mistake, but it keeps paying that 10% after the winner is obvious.

- **UCB1** — *optimism in the face of uncertainty.* Score each arm by

  $$\hat{\mu}_i + \sqrt{2\ln t / n_i}$$

  and choose the largest score. The first term is what you believe; the second is how wrong you might still be. Barely tried arms get a bonus that shrinks as evidence arrives.

- **KL-UCB.** For Bernoulli feedback—click/no-click, purchase/no-purchase—UCB1's generic Hoeffding radius is conservative. KL-UCB asks a sharper question: *how high could this arm's true Bernoulli mean still plausibly be, given the observations?* It chooses the largest $q$ such that

  $$n_i\,\mathrm{KL}(\hat{\mu}_i\,\|\,q) \leq \ln t + c\ln\ln t$$

  where $\mathrm{KL}$ is Bernoulli KL divergence. Its upper bound follows the shape of binomial evidence, so it is especially tighter near probabilities close to 0 or 1.

- **Thompson sampling** — *probability matching.* Keep a belief distribution for each arm. Each round, draw one plausible value from every belief and choose the highest draw. A barely tried arm has a wide posterior and occasionally gets another chance. A known-bad arm has a narrow, low posterior and rarely wins.

<Demo src="/demos/posts/edp-sort/bandit-race-averaged.en.html" title="Average multi-armed bandit policy race" height={560} />

Run the **hard** setting, where the top two arms are close. This chart averages many deterministic trials, so it shows a policy trend rather than the winner of one lucky rollout. Pure exploit fails because an early lucky result can become a permanent false belief. ε-greedy repairs that by buying random checks. UCB1, KL-UCB, and Thompson improve on it because their checks go where uncertainty is still relevant.

KL-UCB is not a universal replacement for Thompson. It assumes the reward model is close to Bernoulli and pays for a numerical inverse-KL solve. But when that model is the right one, it removes avoidable slack from UCB1's confidence bonus.

```python
import math
import numpy as np


def bernoulli_kl(p, q):
    eps = 1e-12
    p = min(1 - eps, max(eps, p))
    q = min(1 - eps, max(eps, q))
    return p * math.log(p / q) + (1 - p) * math.log((1 - p) / (1 - q))


def kl_ucb(mean, pulls, t, c=3.0):
    if mean >= 1.0:
        return 1.0

    budget = (math.log(max(t, 2)) + c * math.log(math.log(max(t, 3)))) / pulls
    low, high = mean, 1.0 - 1e-12

    for _ in range(32):
        candidate = (low + high) / 2
        if bernoulli_kl(mean, candidate) <= budget:
            low = candidate
        else:
            high = candidate
    return low


def choose_arm(successes, pulls, t, policy, rng):
    unseen = np.flatnonzero(pulls == 0)
    if len(unseen):
        return int(rng.choice(unseen))

    means = successes / pulls

    if policy == "epsilon_greedy":
        return int(rng.integers(len(pulls))) if rng.random() < 0.10 else int(np.argmax(means))

    if policy == "ucb1":
        scores = means + np.sqrt(2.0 * np.log(t) / pulls)
        return int(np.argmax(scores))

    if policy == "kl_ucb":
        scores = np.array([kl_ucb(means[a], pulls[a], t) for a in range(len(pulls))])
        return int(np.argmax(scores))

    if policy == "thompson":
        samples = rng.beta(1 + successes, 1 + pulls - successes)
        return int(np.argmax(samples))


def update(successes, pulls, arm, reward):
    pulls[arm] += 1
    successes[arm] += reward
```

</details>

The good actions are not simply the high-reward ones. They are the actions that are worth both their immediate outcome and what they teach you.

### Context changes which arm is best

The plain bandit assumes each arm has one global mean $\mu_a$. That is often the wrong parameterization.

A quick lunch, a date, and a rainy solo dinner are different **contexts**. A restaurant can be weak for one and exactly right for another. On a product surface, the equivalent context may include query, device, user intent, price band, time of day, or any feature known *before* the decision.

In the simplest disjoint linear contextual bandit, the expected reward for action $a$ in context $x_t$ is

$$\mathbb{E}[r_t \mid x_t, a] = x_t^\top\theta_a$$

The unknown object is no longer one scalar per arm. It is a vector $\theta_a$ for each arm: the way that arm responds across the context space.

For each arm, LinUCB keeps a regularized design matrix and reward vector:

$$A_a = \lambda I + \sum_{s:a_s=a} x_sx_s^\top, \qquad b_a = \sum_{s:a_s=a} r_sx_s$$

It estimates $\hat{\theta}_a=A_a^{-1}b_a$, then chooses the action with the largest optimistic score:

$$x_t^\top\hat{\theta}_a + \alpha\sqrt{x_t^\top A_a^{-1}x_t}$$

The first term is predicted reward. The second is not vague “uncertainty about the restaurant.” It is uncertainty about **this direction in context space**. A restaurant may be well understood for weekday lunch but still uncertain for a high-intent mobile user on a rainy evening.

That is what context buys you: evidence can transfer between related requests. A dinner for one rainy evening does not only update a restaurant's global average; it updates the parts of its response model that nearby contexts share.

There are limits. Every context feature must be available before acting; a post-click feature is leakage, not context. Features should be scaled and regularized. And disjoint LinUCB only transfers evidence *within* an arm. When there are many sparse arms, use a shared or hybrid model—shared user/item features plus an arm-specific residual—or an action-feature model instead.

import numpy as np


class DisjointLinUCB:
    def __init__(self, n_arms, dim, alpha=0.8, l2=1.0):
        self.alpha = alpha
        self.A = [l2 * np.eye(dim) for _ in range(n_arms)]
        self.b = [np.zeros(dim) for _ in range(n_arms)]

    def choose_arm(self, x):
        scores = []
        for A_a, b_a in zip(self.A, self.b):
            theta_a = np.linalg.solve(A_a, b_a)
            predicted_reward = x @ theta_a
            uncertainty = np.sqrt(x @ np.linalg.solve(A_a, x))
            scores.append(predicted_reward + self.alpha * uncertainty)
        return int(np.argmax(scores))

    def update(self, arm, x, reward):
        self.A[arm] += np.outer(x, x)
        self.b[arm] += reward * x

## Part II — Sorting: when the arm is a pair

Eight players. No stat sheet. No clean skill number to read.

You can make any two play and watch who wins. A match is a coin whose bias depends on the skill gap: a strong player usually beats a weak one; evenly matched players are close to a toss-up.

How many matches do you need before the league table is reliable? More importantly: **which match should happen next?**

<Demo src="/demos/posts/edp-sort/pitch-match.html?lang=en&v=3" title="A league table built from noisy 1v1 matches" height={660} />

This is sorting, except the comparison oracle is unreliable. In a textbook, you sort by reading numbers. Here, the better player only *probably* wins.

The interaction is a **dueling bandit**: choose two options, observe only a relative preference, then decide which pair to ask about next. A dueling bandit is the learning problem, not a particular statistical model.

**Bradley-Terry is one useful model inside that broader family.** It assumes every player has one latent scalar skill and that pairwise probabilities come from their difference:

$$P(i \succ j) = \sigma(s_i-s_j)$$

Each player has a hidden skill $s_i$. The logistic curve turns the difference $s_i-s_j$ into a win probability. Equal skills give a 50/50 match; a large gap gives an almost certain result. The absolute score scale is conventional; writing a separate inverse-temperature $\beta$ is equivalent to rescaling all of the skills.

This shared latent structure is why evidence transfers. Match outcomes can be conditionally independent given the skill vector, yet the pairwise probabilities are coupled: if A beats B repeatedly and B beats C repeatedly, you learn something about A versus C without scheduling that match.

Bradley-Terry is not required for a dueling bandit. Other dueling-bandit algorithms may assume only that a Condorcet or Copeland winner exists, or work with a more general pairwise-preference matrix. And some worlds violate every global scalar ranking: rock-paper-scissors cycles, context-specific preferences, and item interactions cannot be faithfully represented by one score per item.

Up to constants, distinguishing a small skill gap $\Delta$ with confidence $1-\delta$ takes roughly

$$O\!\left(\frac{\log(1/\delta)}{\Delta^2}\right)$$

comparisons. Big gaps settle quickly. Near ties consume the budget.

### How the table learns

**Batch fit.** Given all matches so far, find the Bradley-Terry skill values that best explain them. Sort the fitted skills and you have a ranking. This is clean, but refitting from scratch after every match does not scale.

**Online fit: Elo.** Treat each match as one logistic-regression example and take a single SGD step. If $i$ beats $j$:

$$s_i \leftarrow s_i + \eta(1-p), \qquad s_j \leftarrow s_j - \eta(1-p), \qquad p = \sigma(s_i-s_j)$$

The update is the surprise, $1-p$. If the favourite wins, the ratings barely move. If an underdog wins, they move sharply.

<details>
<summary><strong>Click to reveal: Bradley–Terry batch fitting and online Elo</strong></summary>

```python
import numpy as np


def fit_bradley_terry_mm(wins, iterations=100, eps=1e-9):
    """
    wins[i, j] is the number of times item i beat item j.
    Returns log-strengths; sorting them gives the Bradley–Terry ranking.
    """
    wins = np.asarray(wins, dtype=float)
    pair_games = wins + wins.T
    total_wins = wins.sum(axis=1)
    n_items = len(wins)
    strength = np.ones(n_items)

    for _ in range(iterations):
        updated = strength.copy()
        for i in range(n_items):
            denominator = 0.0
            for j in range(n_items):
                if i != j and pair_games[i, j] > 0:
                    denominator += pair_games[i, j] / (strength[i] + strength[j])

            if denominator > 0:
                updated[i] = max(total_wins[i], eps) / denominator

        # Bradley–Terry is identifiable only up to an additive constant in log-space.
        updated /= np.exp(np.mean(np.log(np.maximum(updated, eps))))
        strength = updated

    return np.log(np.maximum(strength, eps))


def elo_update(rating, i, j, winner, learning_rate=0.25):
    """One online logistic-regression step after i versus j."""
    p_i_wins = 1.0 / (1.0 + np.exp(-(rating[i] - rating[j])))
    target_i = 1.0 if winner == i else 0.0
    delta = learning_rate * (target_i - p_i_wins)

    rating[i] += delta
    rating[j] -= delta
    return rating


# Batch: scores = fit_bradley_terry_mm(wins)
# Online: rating = elo_update(rating, i, j, winner)
```

</details>

This bare batch fit assumes the comparison graph is connected. In production, add regularization or a prior: disconnected groups, undefeated items, and winless items can otherwise make unconstrained Bradley-Terry estimates unstable or diverge.

The recipe has three parts:

> **estimate** skills from matches → **read off** the ranking by sorting → **schedule** the next match.

### Where to spend the matches

With a calibrated model, the expected **signed** Elo update is zero for every pairing. The question is not whether a pairing has a nonzero expected update; it is where an observation is most informative.

A lopsided match is usually low-information: the expected favourite winning produces a small update, while an upset produces a large update but happens rarely. Pairwise outcome variance, and therefore local information about the order, is largest near $p=0.5$.

Sample near the boundaries where the answer can still change.

- **Random** chooses any pair.
- **Round-robin** chooses the least-played pair, $\arg\min_{(i,j)}n_{ij}$.
- **Ladder** compares neighbours in the current table.
- **Active** chooses a pair that is both close and under-sampled:

  $$\arg\max_{(i,j)}\left[\text{closeness}(|\hat{s}_i-\hat{s}_j|)+\text{uncertainty}\left(\frac{1}{1+n_{ij}}\right)\right]$$

<Demo src="/demos/posts/edp-sort/pairing-race.html?lang=en&v=3" title="Where to spend your matches" height={440} />

The curve is Kendall $\tau$, or table correctness, against matches played. Boundary-seeking schedulers settle the table first because they spend their budget where the order is still movable.

Set the table to **you scout (click 2)**. Pick two players, run a match, inspect the table, and choose the next pair. Obvious mismatches feel safe, but they rarely change anything.

### Why the candidate pool must be wider than K

Suppose you only need the **best K** items. Comparing only items currently in the top-K fails in three ways.

1. A genuinely top-K item currently sitting at K+1 receives no matches and has no path back into contention.
2. Inside a settled top-K, comparisons are often lopsided; the cutline does not move.
3. If the top group is isolated from the field, “K-th best” loses its anchor.

The fix is a **candidate band around the cutline**. Keep sampling everyone whose uncertainty still overlaps the K-th position. Let the band narrow only when evidence rules people out.

<details>
<summary><strong>Click to reveal: active matching around a top-K cutline</strong></summary>

```python
import numpy as np


def candidate_band(scores, score_se, k, z=1.96):
    """
    Keep candidates whose confidence intervals overlap the current K-th item.
    score_se can come from a Hessian approximation, a bootstrap, or a posterior.
    """
    order = np.argsort(-scores)
    cutline_item = order[k - 1]
    cutline_low = scores[cutline_item] - z * score_se[cutline_item]
    cutline_high = scores[cutline_item] + z * score_se[cutline_item]

    lower = scores - z * score_se
    upper = scores + z * score_se
    band = np.flatnonzero((upper >= cutline_low) & (lower <= cutline_high))

    # Keep enough local rivals even when the intervals are overconfident early on.
    local = order[max(0, k - 4):min(len(scores), k + 4)]
    return np.unique(np.concatenate([band, local]))


def choose_active_pair(scores, score_se, pair_games, candidates):
    """Prefer uncertain, close pairs inside the cutline candidate band."""
    best_pair = None
    best_value = -np.inf

    for left in range(len(candidates)):
        for right in range(left + 1, len(candidates)):
            i, j = candidates[left], candidates[right]

            # Lightweight heuristic. A full BTL fit should use uncertainty
            # of the difference s_i - s_j, including covariance.
            pair_se = np.sqrt(score_se[i] ** 2 + score_se[j] ** 2)
            standardized_gap = abs(scores[i] - scores[j]) / (pair_se + 1e-9)
            boundary_value = 1.0 / (1.0 + standardized_gap)
            pair_uncertainty = 1.0 / np.sqrt(pair_games[i, j] + 1.0)
            value = boundary_value * pair_uncertainty

            if value > best_value:
                best_value = value
                best_pair = (i, j)

    return best_pair

# band = candidate_band(elo_rating, score_se, k=100)
# i, j = choose_active_pair(elo_rating, score_se, pair_games, band)
# winner = observe_comparison(i, j)
# elo_rating = elo_update(elo_rating, i, j, winner)
```

The implementation above shows the principle. A cheap production approximation may use a fixed rank radius around K; a better one uses posterior, bootstrap, or Hessian-based uncertainty to decide whose intervals overlap the cutline. For a fitted Bradley-Terry model, the relevant uncertainty is the uncertainty of $s_i-s_j$, including covariance between the two estimated skills.

With **10,000** players there are about 50 million possible pairs. Use an online estimator such as Elo and an adaptive scheduler that focuses comparisons near the K-th rating. The metric is **recall@100**: how much of the true top-100 appears in the estimated top-100.

Adaptive matching recovers most of the top 100 in tens of thousands of matches rather than enumerating all fifty million possible pairs, because it concentrates effort where membership of the set is still uncertain. The exact sample count depends on the gaps around rank 100, outcome noise, model fit, and the stopping rule.

## Part III — Combinatorial bandits: choosing a whole top-K

Everything so far used **comparison feedback**. You stage A against B and receive one bit: who won.

There is another setting where every selected item returns its own outcome: a click, watch time, purchase, or benchmark score. The goal may still be top-K, but the observation model is different.

- **Comparison feedback:** choose a pair, receive “A beat B,” fit or update a preference model, then schedule the next comparison.
- **Per-item reward feedback:** pick a slate and observe a reward for each selected item.

The second setting is a **combinatorial semi-bandit**. For each item, Combinatorial-UCB keeps an optimistic score:

$$u_i = \bar{x}_i + \sqrt{c\,\ln t/n_i}$$

Each round, choose the top-K by $u_i$, observe a reward for each selected item, and update those items.

This simple top-K rule assumes additive per-item rewards. On a real ranked surface, position bias, substitution, cannibalization, diversity constraints, and slate-level interactions can break that assumption. More fundamentally, an observed click is conditional on exposure under the current policy. Randomized exploration, propensity correction, or other counterfactual methods may be needed before a click estimate means what we think it means. Then “choose the top-K UCB scores” is no longer the full optimization problem; the action must be valued as a slate.

The limitation is simpler still: **Comb-UCB cannot run on “who won?”** It needs a reward for each item. A football match does not provide one.

import numpy as np


class CombinatorialUCB:
    def __init__(self, n_items, exploration=1.0):
        self.reward_sum = np.zeros(n_items)
        self.pulls = np.zeros(n_items, dtype=np.int64)
        self.exploration = exploration

    def select_slate(self, k, t):
        means = np.divide(
            self.reward_sum,
            self.pulls,
            out=np.zeros_like(self.reward_sum),
            where=self.pulls > 0,
        )
        bonus = np.full_like(means, np.inf)
        seen = self.pulls > 0
        bonus[seen] = self.exploration * np.sqrt(np.log(max(t, 2)) / self.pulls[seen])
        scores = means + bonus

        # argpartition avoids sorting every item when k << n_items.
        slate = np.argpartition(scores, -k)[-k:]
        return slate[np.argsort(-scores[slate])]

    def update(self, slate, rewards):
        self.pulls[slate] += 1
        self.reward_sum[slate] += np.asarray(rewards)


slate = policy.select_slate(k=12, t=round_number)
rewards = observe_per_item_rewards(slate)  # one reward for every selected item
policy.update(slate, rewards)

## What this adds up to

The restaurant, the league table, and the top-K slate are variations on the same move.

The optimization structure is known. The parameters that determine the answer are not. Attempts reveal them only partially and noisily, so the solver must decide both what to do now and what to learn next.

For independent arms, that is a multi-armed bandit. For pairwise comparisons, it is a dueling bandit; Bradley-Terry is one structural assumption that makes those comparisons transfer into a latent ranking. For a slate with per-item rewards, it is a combinatorial semi-bandit.

You are not just choosing a restaurant, a match, or a slate.

> **You are choosing which ignorance is worth paying to remove.**
