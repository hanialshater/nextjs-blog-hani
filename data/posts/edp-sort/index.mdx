A recommendation team launches a new slot on a high-traffic page.

There are five candidate policies for filling it. Each policy may have a different conversion rate, a different return rate, and a different effect on long-term engagement. None of those quantities is known well enough to trust offline. The only way to learn what policy does is to give it traffic and observe what happens.

That sounds obvious, but it changes the problem completely.

In an ordinary optimization problem, the parameters are known. You estimate the value of every action, take the maximum, and execute:

$$a^* = \arg\max_a V(a; \theta)$$

In this problem, the parameter vector $\theta$ is hidden. Worse: you do not get observations for free. Showing policy $a_t$ produces an outcome $y_t$, and it tells you nothing directly about the policies you did not show. Your decision is therefore also an experiment.

I will call this the **hidden-parameter problem**:

> **The quantities that determine the correct decision are unknown, and the only way to estimate them is to take actions whose consequences reveal partial evidence.**

Different fields give pieces of this problem different names: online learning, sequential experimentation, adaptive control, active ranking, bandits. The names are useful, but they can obscure the shared structure.

The shared structure is simple:

$$\theta \text{ is hidden}; \qquad a_t \text{ produces } y_t \sim P_\theta(y \mid a_t); \qquad a_{t+1} \text{ depends on what happened before.}$$

You are not optimizing *after* learning the parameters. You are learning the parameters *through* optimization.

This post is about how to recognize that kind of problem and solve it. The examples move from a simple bandit to noisy ranking and top-K selection, but the point is not to collect algorithm names. It is to learn what to ask when the answer has to be discovered by trying.

## The four questions that define the problem

Before choosing an algorithm, answer four questions.

### 1. What exactly is unknown?

Sometimes each action has one hidden reward rate: click-through rate for an item, conversion rate for a ranking policy, defect rate for a manufacturing setting.

Sometimes the unknown is conditional: the right action depends on user, query, device, price band, or time of day. Then the hidden object is not one number per action; it is a response function.

Sometimes the unknowns are linked. In a ranking problem, each item may have a hidden latent score, and a comparison between two items teaches you about both—and indirectly about every other comparison involving them.

Naming the unknown parameters is the first real act of problem solving. Without it, “exploration” becomes an excuse for random traffic allocation.

### 2. What evidence does an action generate?

Feedback is not a detail. It determines what can be learned.

- Show one policy and observe one conversion: **per-action reward feedback**.
- Compare A against B and observe only the winner: **pairwise comparison feedback**.
- Show a slate of ten items and observe a reward for each displayed item: **semi-bandit feedback**.
- Show a slate and observe only total page value: **aggregate feedback**.

These are different information channels. A method built for one should not be casually imported into another.

### 3. What is the actual objective?

There are at least two very different objectives that people often blur together.

- **Cumulative reward:** make good choices while learning. A bad action is costly because it hurts today.
- **Identification:** spend a finite budget to identify the best arm, the correct order, or the top-K set. A bad action is costly mainly because it wastes evidence.

A product surface usually has both. You want revenue now, but you also want to stop being wrong about the policy. The relative weight determines how aggressive exploration should be.

### 4. What structure lets evidence transfer?

The naïve model treats every action as independent. Then a result from item A says nothing about item B.

But most worthwhile problems have structure: user features, item embeddings, a shared latent ranking, graph constraints, or a response model. Structure is how one observation becomes useful beyond the exact action that generated it.

The rest of the post is just this checklist in action.

## Part I — The smallest hidden-parameter problem: a bandit

Start with the deliberately simple version.

A product team has five candidate ranking policies for a slot. Policy $i$ has an unknown probability $\mu_i$ of generating a desired outcome. Each session, you must choose exactly one policy. You see a noisy reward—say, purchase or no purchase—only for the policy you deployed.

There is no replay log containing the counterfactual answer. You cannot ask what would have happened under the four policies you did not show. Their rewards remain unobserved.

This is the **multi-armed bandit**:

- each policy is an **arm**;
- sending a session to it is a **pull**;
- the observed outcome is the **reward**;
- $\mu_i$ is the hidden parameter you are trying to learn.

The restaurant version is the same toy model with tastier rewards: each restaurant has a hidden average quality, every dinner is noisy, and choosing one tells you nothing directly about the others.

### The mistake that creates the problem

If the $\mu_i$ values were visible, there would be no bandit problem. Compute $\max_i \mu_i$ and ship it.

The hard case is not uncertainty in the abstract. The hard case is **selective evidence**: you learn only about what you chose.

That is why a normal A/B test mindset is insufficient. A classical fixed experiment says: allocate traffic evenly, wait, estimate, decide. A bandit says: the decision happens every round, and waiting has an opportunity cost.

The cost is measured by **regret**:

$$\text{Regret}(T) = \sum_{t=1}^{T}\big(\mu^* - \mu_{a_t}\big), \qquad \mu^* = \max_i \mu_i$$

At time $t$, regret is the value you lose by choosing $a_t$ instead of the true best arm. The oracle knows $\mu^*$ from the start. You do not.

So every policy must solve the same tension:

- exploit what currently looks best;
- explore actions whose uncertainty could still change the decision.

Not random exploration. **Decision-relevant exploration.**

The demo uses restaurants, but read it as five unknown policy parameters. The important fact is that a single good or bad outcome is not a verdict. It is evidence with variance.

### Three ways to turn uncertainty into an action

The main policies differ in only one thing: how they convert an estimate and its uncertainty into the next pull.

- **ε-greedy:** choose the current best arm most of the time; reserve a fixed fraction $\varepsilon$ for random exploration. It is robust and easy, but spends exploration budget even when uncertainty is already resolved.
- **UCB1:** choose the arm with the highest optimistic estimate:

  $$\hat{\mu}_i + \sqrt{2\ln t / n_i}$$

  The first term is what you currently believe. The second is the uncertainty bonus. An arm sampled only a few times is allowed to look better than its mean because it might be better. The bonus shrinks as evidence accumulates.

- **Thompson sampling:** maintain a distribution over plausible values of each $\mu_i$. Sample one plausible world, choose the best arm in that world, observe the result, update the distribution. Wide uncertainty naturally creates occasional exploration; narrow, low posteriors stop winning.

<Demo src="/demos/posts/edp-sort/bandit-race.html" title="How exploration policies spend evidence" height={560} />

The useful lesson from this race is not that one curve is universally superior. It is that pure exploitation fails for a specific reason: a lucky early reward can become a permanent false belief. ε-greedy repairs that by buying random checks. UCB and Thompson do better because they spend their checks where uncertainty is still material.

<Demo src="/demos/posts/edp-sort/bandit-playground.html" title="Choose the next pull yourself" height={520} />

### Context changes the parameterization

The independent-arm model is often too crude.

On a retail site, “policy A has conversion rate 3%” is rarely the parameter you care about. Policy A may work for high-intent users on mobile, fail for gift shoppers, and interact with price sensitivity. The hidden quantity is closer to:

$$\mu_i(x) = \text{expected reward of action } i \text{ in context } x$$

A **contextual bandit** observes $x$ before acting. In LinUCB, for example, each arm learns a weight vector $\theta_i$ and predicts $x_t^\top\theta_i$, then adds an uncertainty bonus. The action is no longer “which policy is globally best?” It is “which policy is best for this request, given what we know and what we still need to learn?”

<Demo src="/demos/posts/edp-sort/bandit-contextual.html" title="Contextual bandit: unknown response functions" height={420} />

This is the first structural upgrade. Evidence from one mobile, high-intent session can inform nearby sessions in feature space. You are not merely counting rewards per arm anymore; you are fitting a response model.

### What the bandit taught us

A bandit is not fundamentally “a restaurant choice problem.” It is the smallest model of a hidden-parameter problem:

1. each action has an unknown consequence;
2. acting produces the only evidence;
3. evidence is partial and noisy;
4. the next action should depend on what has been learned.

Now change one thing: suppose you cannot observe a reward for an item at all. You can only compare two items and hear which one won.

That is a ranking problem.

## Part II — Hidden parameters with shared structure: ranking from comparisons

Suppose a marketplace has eight new sellers with no reliable transaction history. You need a quality order, but the only available signal is a noisy comparison: experts or customers can look at two sellers and say which one seems stronger.

You do not observe “seller A has quality 0.73.” You observe statements like “A beat B.”

This is a different feedback channel, but it is still a hidden-parameter problem. The hidden parameters are now the latent skills:

$$s_1, s_2, \ldots, s_n$$

A comparison between $i$ and $j$ is generated by their difference. The standard Bradley-Terry model writes:

$$P(i \succ j) = \sigma\!\big(\beta(s_i-s_j)\big)$$

Equal skills give a 50/50 comparison. A large skill gap makes the stronger item likely to win. The parameter $\beta$ controls how decisive the comparisons are.

<Demo src="/demos/posts/edp-sort/pitch-match.html" title="Build a ranking from noisy comparisons" height={660} />

The football presentation is useful because it makes the observation model visible. A single match does not measure skill. It supplies one noisy constraint on a pairwise difference.

### This is not just many bandit arms

At the level of raw outcomes, each pairwise result is Bernoulli: A wins or B wins.

But the pairs share parameters. If A beats B repeatedly and B beats C repeatedly, you have learned something about A versus C without ever scheduling that match. This transitive transfer is the central structural advantage of ranking.

Treat every pair as an independent bandit arm and you throw that advantage away. The right model estimates one global vector of skills and lets evidence propagate through it.

### Estimation: recover the latent scores

There are two useful forms.

**Batch Bradley-Terry fit.** Given all comparisons so far, find the skill vector that makes the observed wins most likely. The eight-player demo uses a few minorize-maximize passes. Sort the fitted skills and you have a current table.

**Online fit: Elo.** When comparisons arrive continuously, do not refit the entire model after every match. Update the two participating scores once. If $i$ beats $j$:

$$s_i \leftarrow s_i + \eta(1-p), \qquad s_j \leftarrow s_j - \eta(1-p), \qquad p=\sigma(s_i-s_j)$$

The magnitude $1-p$ is the surprise. If an expected favourite wins, there is little to learn and ratings move little. If an underdog wins, the model moves sharply.

Elo is not magic. It is an online estimator for a latent-parameter model.

### Experiment design: choose the next comparison

Once the estimator exists, the main problem returns: which pair should you compare next?

A comparison between items with a large estimated gap is often wasteful. It confirms an order that is already almost certain. A comparison between near-equal items may resolve an ordering that actually affects the table.

The sample cost makes this precise. If two skills differ by $\Delta$, identifying their order with confidence $1-\delta$ requires roughly:

$$\log(1/\delta) / \Delta^2$$

comparisons. Large gaps settle quickly. Near ties consume the evidence budget.

That gives a simple operational rule:

> **Sample near the boundaries where the answer can still change.**

<Demo src="/demos/posts/edp-sort/pairing-race.html" title="Ranking is also an experiment-design problem" height={440} />

The schedulers expose different beliefs about where evidence matters.

- **Random** spreads evidence everywhere, including settled blowouts.
- **Round-robin** gives every pair equal coverage, which is fair but often inefficient.
- **Ladder** compares neighbours in the current ranking, because adjacent items are likely to be uncertain.
- **Active scheduling** combines closeness and uncertainty, explicitly choosing the comparison most likely to improve the table.

The active policy is not using a fancier sort. It is using the comparison budget better.

### The top-K objective changes where the boundary is

Usually you do not need a perfect ranking of everything. You need the best $K$ items.

That changes the experimental target. The important uncertainty is no longer every adjacent pair. It is the region around the K-th position—the **cutline**.

The common mistake is to compare only items currently inside the top-K. That creates three failures.

1. **Feedback-loop selection bias.** A genuinely top-K item currently sitting at K+1 receives no comparisons, so it has no route back into contention.
2. **Dead updates.** Comparisons among already separated top items are often predictable, so Elo updates are near zero. They do not move the cutline.
3. **Disconnected evidence.** If the top group stops being compared with the field, the scale loses its anchor. “K-th best” becomes less identifiable.

The fix is a **candidate band** around the cutline. Keep sampling everyone whose uncertainty overlaps the K-th position. Narrow the band only after the evidence rules people out.

<Demo src="/demos/posts/edp-sort/topk-scale.html" title="10,000 items: recover the real top 100" height={500} />

At ten thousand items there are almost fifty million possible pairs. The point of the adaptive scheduler is not to search all of them. It is to spend comparisons in the small region where the membership of the top 100 is still uncertain.

This is a general principle of hidden-parameter problem solving: **do not explore where uncertainty is large in the abstract; explore where uncertainty can still change a decision.**

## Part III — The feedback channel decides the machine

Top-K selection appears in another form.

Suppose you show a slate of $K$ products. This time you receive a separate reward for each displayed product: click, add-to-cart, watch time, or conversion. You are not asking an observer “which of these two is better?” You are receiving an outcome for every selected item.

That is not noisy sorting. It is a **combinatorial semi-bandit**.

For each item, Combinatorial-UCB keeps an optimistic score:

$$u_i = \bar{x}_i + \sqrt{c\,\ln t / n_i}$$

Each round, choose the top-K items by $u_i$. Observe rewards for the selected K items. Update those items. The action is combinatorial because it is a whole slate, not one arm.

<Demo src="/demos/posts/edp-sort/bt-vs-combucb.html" title="Same top-K goal, different feedback, different algorithm" height={400} />

The comparison with Bradley-Terry is intentionally uneven. Combinatorial-UCB receives several direct rewards per round. Bradley-Terry receives one comparison bit. It is not a race to crown a winner; it is a demonstration that information geometry determines the learning rate and the appropriate model.

The two systems leave you with different assets too:

- **Bradley-Terry / Elo** builds a global latent ranking. It can answer new top-K queries because it models relative quality across all items.
- **Combinatorial-UCB** learns per-item reward estimates for the items it has selected. It is efficient when the feedback really is per item, but it does not magically infer pairwise structure from absent data.

Same product goal. Different observation model. Different solution.

## A practical recipe for hidden-parameter problem solving

When you meet a problem that seems to require “trying things,” do not jump straight to a bandit library. Work through this sequence.

### 1. Write down the hidden parameters

What would be known if you had perfect information?

- a reward rate per action;
- a response function per context;
- a latent score per item;
- an interaction matrix between pairs or slate elements;
- a changing state that must be tracked over time.

This tells you what the model is trying to learn.

### 2. Write down the observation model

For a chosen action, what arrives back?

Not “we have logs.” Be precise. Do you observe a reward for the chosen item, the full slate, the page total, a pairwise winner, or a delayed outcome? Which counterfactuals remain invisible?

Most bad algorithm choices start here: they assume a richer feedback channel than the product actually supplies.

### 3. Separate learning objective from business objective

Are you trying to maximize cumulative value during learning? Identify the best policy by a deadline? Recover the top 100? Maintain a ranking as preferences drift?

The answer determines the correct metric: regret, simple regret, recall@K, Kendall $\tau$, calibration, or a risk-sensitive variant. Do not optimize a generic “accuracy” number when the decision is about a cutline.

### 4. Find the structure that transfers evidence

Independent arms are the fallback, not the default.

Use context when nearby requests should behave similarly. Use latent-score models when pairwise comparisons are generated by shared item quality. Use slate models when items interact. Use graphs when actions share components.

Structure is not decorative modeling. It is what lowers the cost of exploration.

### 5. Define the decision boundary

Which uncertainty could alter what you actually do next?

For a best-arm problem, it is the gap between the top contenders. For a top-K problem, it is the band around K. For a policy rollout, it is the decision threshold for shipping or stopping. For a ranking page, it may be the items that can still enter a visible position.

Spend evidence there.

### 6. Keep exploration safe enough to survive contact with production

The mathematically best information-gathering action is not always acceptable. You may need constraints: minimum relevance, maximum exposure loss, segment protection, delayed-reward correction, conservative priors, or explicit holdouts.

That does not make the hidden-parameter problem disappear. It makes the policy constrained. The core loop remains: act, observe partial evidence, update, allocate the next action.

## The point

The deep distinction is not between “machine learning problems” and “algorithm problems.” It is between problems whose parameters are available before you act and problems whose parameters are created by acting.

For the first kind, solve and execute.

For the second kind, there is no clean separation. Your solver must decide what to do **and** what to learn next. Bandits are the simplest version. Contextual bandits add shared response structure. Noisy ranking adds latent parameters and comparison feedback. Combinatorial bandits add whole actions and per-item rewards.

The reusable idea is smaller than all of those names:

> **When the answer depends on hidden parameters, and actions are how you reveal them, problem solving is the design of a good sequence of tries.**

The best next try is not simply the one with the highest estimated value. It is the one whose value and information are jointly worth its cost.
