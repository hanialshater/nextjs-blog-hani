Most algorithms are taught in a small, unusually generous universe.

The inputs are already there. The numbers are trustworthy. You sort them, optimize them, take the maximum, and go home. Five restaurants with ratings of 4.8, 4.3, 4.1, 3.9, and 3.7? Take the first one. No drama.

Real decisions are ruder than that.

Imagine five restaurants nearby and a guest you would like to impress. You can try one restaurant tonight. Tomorrow you can try another. But each dinner is a messy piece of evidence: perhaps the chef had a bad night, perhaps you ordered the wrong thing, perhaps the place is excellent on Tuesdays and terrible on Saturdays. A good meal is evidence. A bad meal is evidence too. Neither is a verdict.

So the problem is no longer “which score is largest?” It is:

> **How do I discover what is best without spending too many attempts on choices that hurt?**

That question starts with dinner, but it travels remarkably far. It governs which ad to show, which product widget to test, which player to compare, which candidates deserve another interview, and how to recover the best hundred items from a population of ten thousand when all you can afford is a tiny fraction of the possible evidence.

This post follows that one question through three increasingly strange worlds:

1. choosing one uncertain option at a time;
2. constructing a ranking when the only measurement is a noisy head-to-head match;
3. choosing a whole top-K slate when every selected item produces its own reward.

The vocabulary changes. The underlying tension does not:

> **Explore or exploit: where should the next try go?**

## Part I — The bandit problem: choosing dinner

The restaurant problem is a **multi-armed bandit**. Choose one option, observe one noisy outcome, update what you believe, repeat.

The difficult part is not choosing. The difficult part is deciding how many mediocre dinners you are willing to buy in order to learn where the good dinners are.

The terminology is just the dinner story wearing a lab coat. Each restaurant is an **arm**. A visit is a **pull**. The **reward** is whether the meal was good enough that you would gladly return. You never observe a restaurant’s true average quality. You observe one noisy dinner at a time—and only at the place you chose. Every unvisited restaurant remains a counterfactual: you do not get to see what you missed.

### When the numbers are visible, the problem is boring

Give me five scores—0.3, 0.7, 0.5, 0.4, 0.6—and “find the best” is a five-step scan.

Hide the scores and it becomes interesting. Each dinner is a noisy sample from a quality you cannot inspect directly. One visit tells you *something*, but not enough. You may have one hundred dinners, or ten thousand impressions, or one finite experiment budget.

The question is not merely what to choose. It is **in what order to learn**.

### The price of ignorance: regret

This is the shape of every repeated decision under uncertainty: where to eat, which email subject line to send, which recommendation module to show. Formally, there are $k$ arms, each with an unknown reward distribution. You pull one arm per round, observe a noisy reward, and want to maximize the total reward you collect.

The same goal is usually phrased as minimizing **regret**: the gap between your choices and an oracle that somehow knew the best arm from the beginning.

$$\text{Regret}(T) = \sum_{t=1}^{T}\big(\mu^* - \mu_{a_t}\big), \qquad \mu^* = \max_i \mu_i$$

Regret is the quality you left on the table. If Sushi is truly the best restaurant and you eat somewhere weaker tonight, the gap between their hidden averages is tonight’s expected regret.

And there is no painless answer. Commit after a handful of pulls and early noise will often crown the wrong winner. Explore every restaurant forever and you will keep paying for bad meals long after the answer is obvious. **Every bandit algorithm is simply a rule for dividing your budget between finding out and cashing in.**

Before naming the algorithms, try the feeling yourself. There are five restaurants. Their real quality is hidden. You choose where to eat; a mysterious policy is choosing in parallel. Your job is not to win one dinner. It is to avoid accumulating painful dinners while you learn.

After a few rounds, reveal the policy. If it feels as though it is exploring in a way that is neither random nor fully committed, you have already met the central idea.

### Three policies cover most of the territory

The first surprise is that the dumb-sounding policies work extremely well. The sophisticated ones win by less than people expect.

- **ε-greedy.** Play the best-so-far arm 90% of the time; play a random arm for the other 10%. The randomness is a tax you pay to avoid being trapped by an early mistake. It is hard to break, but it keeps wasting that 10% even once the winner is obvious.
- **UCB1** — *optimism in the face of uncertainty.* Score each arm by $\hat{\mu}_i + \sqrt{2\ln t / n_i}$ and pick the maximum: what you know, plus how wrong you might still be. Barely sampled arms receive a bonus that shrinks as evidence arrives. Exploration is therefore **directed** at real uncertainty rather than sprayed evenly across everything.
- **Thompson sampling** — *probability matching.* Keep a belief distribution for each arm—a Beta distribution for win/loss rewards. Each round, draw one plausible value from each belief and choose the highest draw. A rarely tried arm has a wide posterior, so it occasionally looks brilliant and gets another chance. A known-bad arm has a narrow, low posterior and almost never wins. Exploration fades by itself. It is embarrassingly effective in practice.

What they share is tiny: start with a weak prior, choose, pull, update **only the arm you pulled**, repeat. What differs is only the mechanism that turns “estimate plus uncertainty” into the next action.

<Demo src="/demos/posts/edp-sort/bandit-race.html" title="Race the bandit policies" height={560} />

Race ε-greedy, UCB, and Thompson on the **hard** setting, where the top two arms are nearly tied. Then race ε-greedy against **pure exploit** on **balanced**. The shape of the story becomes visible fast:

- pure exploitation locks on to an early accident almost as often as it finds the right arm;
- ε-greedy wins because a 10% exploration tax is cheaper than freezing a bad decision;
- UCB and Thompson improve on ε-greedy because their exploration has somewhere specific to go;
- UCB and Thompson usually exchange a narrow, noisy lead.

Bandits are rarely where you find your grand competitive edge. Getting the feedback loop right matters far more than winning an ideological war between two good policies.

A small clarification about the claim that “UCB is slow”: it is not computationally slow. It is one index per arm. The slowness is **learning** slowness. UCB1’s $\sqrt{2\ln t / n_i}$ term is a deliberately conservative confidence radius, valid for any bounded reward, so it keeps checking arms a more model-aware method might already abandon. In production, people often use tighter variants such as KL-UCB or UCB-Tuned, or Thompson sampling when a Bayesian reward model is natural.

### Context changes which arm is best

There is one immediate complication from real life: the best restaurant depends on the night. A quick lunch, a date, and a rainy solo dinner are different **contexts**.

A plain bandit learns one global average per restaurant. A **contextual bandit** sees features of the situation *before* choosing. **LinUCB** is UCB with that additional layer: instead of one mean per arm, each arm learns weights $\theta_a$ over a context vector $x_t$, predicts $x_t^\top \theta_a$, and adds the same optimism bonus. The same restaurant can be a terrible lunch choice and the perfect place for a rainy night.

<Demo src="/demos/posts/edp-sort/bandit-contextual.html" title="The contextual dinner bandit" height={420} />

### The whole first act in one sentence

*A little cleverness wins by a lot; much more cleverness wins by a little.*

ε-greedy, UCB, and Thompson sit on a smooth curve. Any of them is vastly better than committing too early or never committing at all. For the textbook setup—independent arms, one pull per round, immediate reward—the core problem is largely solved.

But it is also a very particular setup. What happens when the thing you pull is not one item but a **pair**, and the only feedback you receive is which member of the pair won?

Then we are no longer choosing dinner. We are sorting under noise.

## Part II — Sorting when the arm is a pair

Eight players. No stat sheet. No clean skill number to read.

You can make any two play and watch who wins. A single match is a coin whose bias depends on the skill gap: a great player usually beats a weak player; two evenly matched players produce something close to a toss-up.

How many matches do you need before the league table is reliable? More importantly: **which match should happen next?**

<Demo src="/demos/posts/edp-sort/pitch-match.html" title="A league table built from noisy 1v1 matches" height={660} />

True skill is hidden on the left; the current estimate is on the right. Press **reveal true skill** at any moment to see how close the table is. Change the **scheduler** to choose who plays—or switch to **you scout** and run the league yourself.

This is sorting, except the comparison oracle is unreliable. In a textbook, you sort by reading numbers. Here, the only way to compare two players is to stage a match, and the better player only *probably* wins. That is the **noisy comparison** problem, also called a **dueling-bandit** problem.

It transforms “what is the order?” into a more operational question:

> **Where should I spend my matches?**

We will build the answer in three moves: a noise model for how matches become evidence, an estimator that turns evidence into a ranking, and a scheduler that chooses the next match. Then we will see why a system that only cares about the top of the ranking behaves differently again.

### Act 1 — Sorting when you can read the numbers

Mergesort, quicksort, heapsort: $O(n \log n)$ comparisons against an exact oracle that says “x is larger” with certainty. The textbook story was effectively closed in 1962.

Every comparison is decisive. You never need to repeat one. There is nothing to estimate.

Remove that certainty and everything changes.

### Act 2 — The oracle is a match, not a measurement

Now the eight objects might be footballers, wines, ad creatives, chess players—anything with a real but unobservable quality. You cannot inspect the quality. You can only stage a contest, and the contest is noisy.

A lopsided match goes the expected way almost every time. An even match is close to a coin flip. The standard model for that coin is the **Bradley-Terry** law:

$$P(i \succ j) = \sigma\!\big(\beta\,(s_i - s_j)\big)$$

Each player has a hidden skill $s_i$. The logistic curve $\sigma$ turns the difference $s_i - s_j$ into a win probability. The parameter $\beta$ says how decisive the game is.

The intuition lives entirely in the gap:

- equal skill, $s_i = s_j$, gives a fair 50/50 match;
- a large gap pushes the probability close to certainty;
- one match is one noisy sample of one pairwise gap.

That last line is the bridge back to bandits. Sorting under noise is like running $n(n-1)/2$ Bernoulli arms in parallel and deciding which pair should receive the next observation.

The cost of confidence follows directly. A pair separated by a gap $\Delta$ needs roughly $\log(1/\delta)/\Delta^2$ repeated matches before you can call the order with confidence $1-\delta$. Blowouts settle themselves. **Near ties consume the budget.**

Keep that fact. It decides everything about scheduling.

### How the table learns: from a batch fit to one update per match

We have match results. We want hidden skills. There are two useful routes, and the second is what survives at scale.

**Batch fit—the eight-player table.** Given all matches so far, find the skill values that best explain them: maximum likelihood under Bradley-Terry. The widget uses a few *minorize-maximize* passes. You do not need the derivation; the picture is enough. It nudges skills until each player’s predicted win rate agrees with the matches they have actually won. Sort the fitted skills and you have a ranking.

That is clean, but refitting from scratch after every new result becomes hopeless once there are thousands of players.

**Online fit = Elo = one gradient step per match.** Treat a match as one logistic-regression example and make one SGD update. When $i$ beats $j$:

$$s_i \leftarrow s_i + \eta\,(1-p), \qquad s_j \leftarrow s_j - \eta\,(1-p), \qquad p = \sigma(s_i - s_j)$$

That is Elo, with step size $\eta$ playing the role of Elo’s $K$.

The update is the surprise: $1-p$. When the favourite wins, $p \approx 1$, so ratings barely move. Nothing surprising happened. When an underdog wins, $p$ is small and ratings move sharply. The system keeps correcting itself toward what the matches say, one $O(1)$ update at a time.

So the noisy-ranking recipe has three separable dials:

> **estimate** skills from matches → **read off** a ranking by sorting → **schedule** the next match.

You can change the estimator without changing the scheduler. You can change the scheduler without changing the estimator. The next question is that third dial, because the matches you feed into Elo determine how quickly the table becomes correct.

### Act 3 — Where the next match should go

The $\Delta^2$ budget tells us what matters. Look again at the Elo update, $\eta(1-p)$:

- A **lopsided** match, where $p \approx 1$, generates an update close to zero. It confirms something you mostly knew.
- A **near-even** match creates a meaningful update and concerns an order that is genuinely uncertain.

**Informative matches are close matches near the decision boundary.** A good scheduler spends evidence there.

Four policies, same league:

- **Random** — choose any pair. Easy to implement, but spends as many matches confirming blowouts as resolving coin flips.
- **Round-robin** — play the least-sampled pair, $\arg\min_{(i,j)} n_{ij}$. Fair coverage, but blind: it keeps replaying settled blowouts.
- **Ladder** — only play players adjacent in the current ranking. The uncertainty is usually between neighbours, not between #1 and #8, so this aims toward the boundary. It reaches a good table in about 1.5× fewer matches than random, but it trusts a noisy current ranking to decide who is adjacent.
- **Active** — play the closest, least-settled pair: $\arg\max_{(i,j)}[\text{closeness}(|\hat{s}_i-\hat{s}_j|) + \text{uncertainty}(1/(1+n_{ij}))]$. It explicitly routes each match toward the place where it can buy the most table-correctness.

This is explore/exploit in its purest form. A match on a settled pair is exploitation without much information. A match on an uncertain pair is exploration that can change the answer.

<Demo src="/demos/posts/edp-sort/pairing-race.html" title="Where to spend your matches" height={440} />

The curve is Kendall $\tau$, or table correctness, against matches played. Whichever policy reaches the top first settles the league first. The boundary-seekers win because they spend the budget where the ranking can still move.

### Now be the scout

The wasted matches become obvious. They are the blowouts you were already expecting. The valuable ones are close calls near the places where the order matters.

### Why the candidate pool must be wider than K

Suppose you do not want the whole ranking. You only want the **best K**.

The tempting shortcut is to schedule matches only inside the current top-K. It fails in three separate ways.

1. **Selection bias: the feedback loop.** A truly top-K player who is currently underrated at rank K+1 never receives matches, so they never get a chance to climb. A noisy ranking starts deciding who is allowed to generate evidence, then freezes itself in place.
2. **Dead gradients.** Inside a settled top-K, many matches are lopsided, so the Elo update $\eta(1-p)$ is nearly zero. Nothing changes. The matches that actually move the cutline are close ones that *straddle* it.
3. **Broken identifiability.** Bradley-Terry and Elo skills are only meaningful while the graph of who played whom remains connected. A top-K-only schedule disconnects the top group from the rest of the field, so “K-th place” loses its anchor.

The cure is the thing you would invent from first principles in a real league: maintain a **candidate band around the cutline**. Keep everyone whose uncertainty still overlaps K-th place, and allow the band to shrink only as confidence grows.

Too tight—exactly K—and all three failures appear. Too wide—the entire field—and you are back to round-robin, wasting evidence on decisions that are already made. That shrinking candidate band is **successive elimination**: retain contenders until the data rules them out.

### Eight players was the tutorial. Now make it ten thousand.

With eight players, you can nearly play everyone. With **10,000**, you cannot. There are $n(n-1)/2 \approx 50$ million possible pairs.

So we use both ideas from above:

- **Estimator:** Elo, the $O(1)$ online update. Re-fitting Bradley-Terry over 10,000 players after every match is hopeless.
- **Scheduler:** adaptive matchmaking that estimates the K-th rating—the cutline—and pairs players near it.

The metric is **recall@100**: how much of the true top-100 your estimated top-100 has recovered.

<Demo src="/demos/posts/edp-sort/topk-scale.html" title="10,000 players: find the best 100" height={500} />

Each match is a noisy Bradley-Terry duel scored by Elo. Adaptive matchmaking races random pairing on recall@100, while the scatter shows estimated rating against true skill and the top-100 cutline.

Random pairing spends itself on blowouts far from the line. Adaptive matching recovers most of the top 100 in tens of thousands of matches rather than fifty million—not by discovering a cleverer rating system, but by putting the evidence budget in the narrow band where the answer is still uncertain.

## Part III — Combinatorial bandits: choosing a whole top-K

Everything so far lived in a world of **comparison feedback**. You stage A against B and only observe a single bit: who won.

There is another world. Every item you try gives back its own number: a click, watch time, a benchmark score, a conversion. Top-K appears in both worlds, but they are not the same problem and they need different machinery.

- **Comparison feedback:** Bradley-Terry / Elo active sorting. Fit skills from “A beat B,” sort them, and schedule the boundary.
- **Per-item reward feedback:** **Combinatorial-UCB**. This is the UCB idea from Part I expanded from one arm to a whole slate.

For each item, Combinatorial-UCB keeps a running mean plus an optimism bonus:

$$u_i = \bar{x}_i + \sqrt{c\,\ln t / n_i}$$

Each round, it chooses the **top-K by $u_i$**. That is the combinatorial part: the action is a complete K-set rather than one item. Then it observes a reward for **each** selected item—called *semi-bandit feedback*—and updates only those items.

The honest catch is also the important one: **Comb-UCB cannot run on “who won.”** It needs one reward per item. A football match does not provide that.

So the next panel is not Comb-UCB “solving” the noisy-sorting problem. It is two different machines looking at the same hidden item quality through different feedback channels: one receives comparison bits; the other receives per-item rewards.

<Demo src="/demos/posts/edp-sort/bt-vs-combucb.html" title="Bradley-Terry versus combinatorial-UCB: top-K recovery" height={400} />

These are averaged curves from a 40-run experiment: $N=30$, $K=5$, 1,000 rounds. Both reach roughly 0.90 top-K accuracy by round 1,000. The early difference tells the real story: by round 100, Comb-UCB is around 0.80 while Bradley-Terry is around 0.59, because Comb-UCB gets **five** rewards per round while Bradley-Terry extracts **one** comparison bit.

It is deliberately not a fair race. That is the lesson.

The artifacts are different too. Bradley-Terry leaves you with a calibrated ranking of all 30 items and a latent model of *why* they are ordered—a result you can reuse for any K. Comb-UCB leaves you with a strong slate of five and per-item means, but says little about items it never selected. Same top-K goal. Different feedback. Different machine.

Choose the machine your signal can actually feed.

## What just happened

Sorting under noise is choose-the-max in disguise. It separates cleanly into three dials:

> **estimate** → **read off** → **schedule**.

Estimate skills from matches. Read off a ranking by sorting. Schedule evidence where the ranking can still change.

The budget question never went away. Should you spend your next observation on something you already believe, or on an uncertainty that could change the decision? That is explore versus exploit. In ranking, the answer is almost always: **the boundary**—widened into a band whenever you only need the top-K.

This is also the first hint that structure changes what can be learned. Independent bandit arms stand alone. Rankings do not. If A beats B and B beats C, then A above C is partly implied for free. That transitive structure becomes even more powerful when the “arms” become edges of a graph and the action becomes a whole path through it.

But that is the next story.
